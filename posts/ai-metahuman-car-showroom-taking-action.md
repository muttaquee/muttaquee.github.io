A few weeks ago I wrote about putting an AI-driven MetaHuman inside a virtual property tour. It was a digital human that could hold a real conversation and follow you from room to room as you explored a home. That was my first case study, and it answered an early question I had: can a virtual human make an immersive experience feel less lonely and a little more human?

Now I am building my second case study, and honestly, this is the part I have been most excited to try. This time the setting is a car showroom, and the MetaHuman is no longer just a guide that talks and follows you around. It can actually do things for you when you ask.

> **The Bigger Picture:** In my first study, the MetaHuman could chat with you and walk beside you. In this one, it can act on what you say. You put on the VR headset, stand next to a car, and say "open the car door for me," and the MetaHuman walks over and opens it. That is the shift I care about: going from a virtual human that only talks to one that also helps.

## From a guide to an assistant

In the property study, anything physical was still on you. The MetaHuman could answer questions, explain a room, and stay by your side, but it could not change anything in the space. If you wanted something to happen, you had to make it happen yourself.

A car showroom asks for something different. Buying a car is hands on. People want to open the doors, sit inside, look under the bonnet, and picture the thing as theirs. So this time I wanted the MetaHuman to feel less like a tour guide and more like a showroom assistant you can actually ask for help.

Here is what the interaction looks like. You are standing next to a car in VR and you say, "open the driver's door for me." The MetaHuman understands what you mean, walks to the right door, and opens it. You did not press a button or point a controller at anything. You just asked, in plain language, and the digital human did it.

## Why this matters

It sounds tiny. A door opens. But the jump underneath that moment is not tiny at all.

For that one small thing to work, the MetaHuman has to pull off a few things at once. It has to keep up a natural conversation like before, understand the intent behind what you said, work out which object in the scene you meant, and then carry out a real action on it. That is the difference between a system that can describe the world and one that can change it for you.

Conversation plus action is what turns a virtual human from a talking presence into something closer to a real assistant. And in a showroom, that lines up almost exactly with what a good salesperson does. They talk with you, they pick up on what you want, and they physically help you experience the product.

## What stays the same, and what changes

The foundation carries over from the first study. Real conversation is still at the heart of it, and the MetaHuman still moves naturally through the space with you. Those were the things I wanted to prove could feel comfortable and believable, and they do.

What changes is agency. The MetaHuman is now taking part in the environment, not just keeping you company inside it. That opens up some genuinely interesting questions:

- Does being able to act on your command make the MetaHuman feel more capable and trustworthy, or does it just raise your expectations to a level that is harder to meet?
- How natural does a spoken command have to be before it feels like talking to a person instead of operating a machine?
- When a digital human physically does something for you, does the whole thing start to feel like a service, and does that change how people engage with the product?

## Why a second industry

Testing the same core technology in a very different setting is on purpose. Real estate and car retail are both big, experience led purchases, but they ask different things of a virtual human. Running the idea across both helps me see what genuinely carries over about AI-driven MetaHumans and what has to be rethought for each context.

If a conversational, action taking MetaHuman can add something real to a home tour and a showroom, that starts to hint at something bigger. It suggests intelligent digital humans could become a general layer of immersive customer experience, rather than a one off trick for a single industry.

## Where this is going

This case study is still in progress, so I am not sharing any results yet. For now it is just the build and the thinking behind it. But the direction feels right. Each step takes the MetaHuman a little closer to something that does not only exist inside a virtual space, but actually helps you while you are in it.
