A few weeks ago I wrote about placing an AI-driven MetaHuman inside a virtual property tour — a digital human that could hold a real-time conversation and follow you from room to room as you explored a home. That was my first case study, and it answered an early question: can a virtual human make an immersive experience feel less lonely and more human?

Now I am building my second case study, and it moves the idea forward in a way I find genuinely exciting. This time the setting is a **car showroom**, and the MetaHuman is no longer just a guide that talks and follows. It can **take action on your command**.

> **The Bigger Picture:** In my first study, the MetaHuman could converse and accompany you. In this one, it can *do things for you*. Say "open the car door for me," and the MetaHuman walks over and opens it. This is the step from a MetaHuman that only talks to one that also acts — from conversation to embodied assistance inside Virtual Reality, where a real person wearing a VR headset gives the command and the virtual human carries it out.

## From a guide to an assistant

In the property study, interaction flowed one way for anything physical. The MetaHuman could answer questions, explain a room, and stay by your side — but it could not *change* the environment. If you wanted to see something happen, you did it yourself.

A car showroom is a different kind of space. Buying a car is a hands-on, high-consideration decision. People want to open the doors, sit inside, look under the bonnet, and picture themselves owning it. So this time I wanted the MetaHuman to behave less like a tour guide and more like a knowledgeable showroom assistant — one you can actually ask to do things.

The interaction I am building looks like this: you are standing beside a car in VR, and you simply say, *"Open the driver's door for me."* The MetaHuman understands the request, walks to the correct door, and opens it. You did not press a button or point a controller — you asked, in natural language, and the digital human acted.

## Why this matters

It sounds small — a door opening — but the leap underneath it is not small at all.

To make that single moment work, the MetaHuman has to do several things together: hold a natural real-time conversation (as before), understand the *intent* behind a spoken command, connect that intent to a specific object in the scene, and then carry out a physical action on it. It is the difference between a system that *describes* the world and one that can *change* it on your behalf.

That combination — conversation plus action — is what turns a virtual human from a talking presence into a genuine assistant. And in a showroom, it maps almost perfectly onto what a real salesperson does: they talk with you, read what you want, and physically help you experience the product.

## What stays the same, what changes

The foundation carries over from the first study. Real-time conversation is still at the heart of the experience, and the MetaHuman still moves naturally through the space with you. Those were the things I wanted to prove could feel comfortable and believable, and they do.

What changes is agency. The MetaHuman is now an active participant in the environment, not just a companion within it. That shift raises new and interesting questions:

- Does being able to *act* on your command make the MetaHuman feel more capable and trustworthy — or does it raise your expectations in ways that are harder to meet?
- How natural does a command have to be before it feels like talking to a person rather than operating a machine?
- When a digital human performs a physical action for you, does the experience feel more like a service, and does that change how people engage with the product?

## Why a second industry

Testing the same core technology in a second, very different domain is deliberate. Real estate and car retail are both high-value, experience-led purchases, but they ask different things of a virtual human. Running the idea across both helps me understand what is genuinely transferable about AI-driven MetaHumans and what has to be redesigned for each context.

If a conversational, action-taking MetaHuman can add value in a home tour *and* a showroom, that starts to say something bigger: that intelligent digital humans could become a general layer of immersive customer experience, not a one-off gimmick for a single industry.

## Where this is going

This case study is still in progress, so I am not sharing results yet — just the build and the thinking behind it. But the direction feels right. Each step is taking the MetaHuman a little closer to something that does not just *inhabit* a virtual space, but genuinely *helps* you inside it.
